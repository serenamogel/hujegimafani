    What does the 7th amendment say in simple terms?

    The 7th Amendment to the U.S. Constitution says that civil cases, or lawsuits based on disagreements between people or businesses, have a right to be decided by a jury in federal court. The amount of the lawsuit must be more than $20, and after a jury settles the case, it shouldn’t go back to trial again.

    What does the 7th amendment do?

    In Suits at common law, where the value in controversy shall exceed twenty dollars, the right of trial by jury shall be preserved, and no fact tried by a jury, shall be otherwise re-examined in any Court of the United States, than according to the rules of the common law.

    Why is the 7th Amendment the most important?

    Why the 7th Amendment Matters To You

    An important aspect of the 7th Amendment is that it prohibits any judge from overruling a jury’s decision, unless there’s a violation of common law at hand. So, in most cases, a jury’s ruling is final.
